Activity Overview

In this activity you'll use systems thinking to explore a real-world issue at your school ‚Äî improving recycling. You'll map how the system works, find the best place to make change, and plan how you can make a difference.

1

Read the Scenario Understand the problem

Your school wants to reduce waste and increase recycling, but many recyclable items are still ending up in the trash. Why is this happening? It's not just one person's fault ‚Äî it's a systems problem with many connected parts.

2

Map the System Identify parts & connections

List the key parts (people, things, organizations) and then show how they connect. Think about who makes decisions, who takes action, and where things can go wrong.

3

Find a Leverage Point Where does change matter most?

A leverage point is a place in the system where a small change can lead to big results. Look at your map ‚Äî where are things breaking down? Where could a nudge create a ripple effect?

4

Choose Your Role How will you contribute?

Pick a role you could realistically play in the system. Think about what you're good at and what you care about. Connect your strengths, values, and goals to the change you want to make.

5

Write Your System Snapshot Tell the story of the system

Write a short narrative (4‚Äì6 sentences) that describes how the system works, where it breaks down, and how your role and actions would improve it.

1

The Scenario What's the problem?

The school wants to reduce waste and increase recycling, but many recyclable items are still being thrown in the trash.

I think this problem exists because the recycling system at school has multiple weak points ‚Äî not just lazy students. The bins might be hard to find, the labels might be confusing, and most students have never been taught what can actually be recycled. It's a system issue, not just a people issue.
2

Map the System Parts & connections

  • Students
  • Teachers
  • Custodial staff
  • Recycling bins & trash bins
  • School administration
  • Local recycling center
1. Students decide whether to throw items in the trash or recycling ‚Äî they are the main "choice point" in the system.
2. Teachers influence student behavior by modeling recycling in classrooms and reinforcing expectations.
3. Administration decides where bins are placed and what signage is used ‚Äî this shapes whether recycling is easy or hard.
4. Custodial staff collects and sorts waste ‚Äî if recycling is contaminated, it may all go to trash anyway.
5. The local recycling center processes materials only if they arrive clean and sorted.
3

Find a Leverage Point Where can change happen?

Student awareness and convenience
  • Many students don't know what can be recycled ‚Äî fixing this changes daily decisions.
  • If bins aren't nearby or clearly labeled, even motivated students won't recycle ‚Äî convenience is key.
  • Students are the biggest group of users in the system, so changing their behavior has the largest impact.
4

Choose Your Role Strengths, values & actions

Green Team Leader (Student Environmental Ambassador)
  • Organized ‚Äî can plan events and coordinate with staff
  • Comfortable speaking in front of groups ‚Äî can present in homeroom
  • Creative ‚Äî can design posters and social media campaigns
  • Environmental responsibility
  • Sustainability
  • Community impact
1. Create simple posters showing what goes in recycling vs. trash bins.
2. Work with administration to place a recycling bin next to every trash can.
3. Give a short presentation in homeroom about why recycling matters.
4. Start a friendly competition between classes to see who recycles the most.
5

System Snapshot Narrative (4‚Äì6 sentences)

Recycling at school depends on how administration sets up the system (bin placement and signage), how teachers reinforce expectations, and how students choose to act. Even if recycling bins exist, the system fails if students are confused or unmotivated. As a Green Team Leader, I would improve the "student awareness and convenience" part of the system. By making recycling easier and clearer ‚Äî through posters, better bin placement, homeroom presentations, and class competitions ‚Äî I would help shift daily habits. Over time, better habits would reduce waste, increase recycling rates, and support a more sustainable school environment.
Rubric Tips

üéØ What to Look For in Student Responses

Strong responses identify at least 4‚Äì5 system parts and explain how they connect. The leverage point is specific and justified with reasoning. The student's role connects their personal strengths and values to a concrete plan of action. The snapshot weaves everything together into a coherent narrative.

Growing responses may list parts without explaining connections, choose a vague leverage point, or describe actions without linking them to the system. Use prompts like: "How does your action change what happens in the system?" or "Who else is affected when you make this change?"
